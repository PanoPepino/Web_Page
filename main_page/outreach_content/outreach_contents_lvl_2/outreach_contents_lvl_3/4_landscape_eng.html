The Landscape

  • Supergravity description comes at a cost; in its simpler forms, it requires ten dimensions (time and nine spatial dimensions) to be free of anomalies.
  • As current observations point only to three large spatial dimensions and time, it is said that the remaining six are compact. A compact dimension is closed, i.e. like the line of a circumference, and way smaller than the usual three dimensions we live in.
  • The process of connecting the ten-dimensional description of supergravity with the four-dimensional one (time + three spatial dimensions) we observe is called compactification. This process translates the properties of the six extra dimensions into properties of the four non-compact ones. In this way, one can say that extra dimensions "decorate" the usual ones.
  • The six extra dimensions can have many different combinations of properties. Depending on the combination, the compactification process will yield different four-dimensional descriptions with many different properties. The set of all possible four-dimensional descriptions provided by this method receives the name of the Landscape of string theory. But which one describes our observable universe?
  • One of the most important properties of the four-dimensional description arising from the compactification process is that of the vacuum energy, which is related to the dark energy. However, obtaining a positive one (Λ > 0), like observations suggest, remains as a really difficult task. Almost all compactifications provide a negative value for the dark energy (Λ < 0).
